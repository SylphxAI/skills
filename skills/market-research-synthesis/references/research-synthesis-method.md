# Research Synthesis Method

## Source matrix

Use multiple evidence surfaces:

- app-store reviews;
- Steam reviews and forums;
- Chrome/Web extension reviews where relevant;
- pricing pages;
- changelogs;
- onboarding flows;
- screenshots and store metadata;
- help centers;
- Reddit/Discord/community complaints;
- comparison pages;
- cancellation/refund policies.

## Extraction table

For each competitor, capture:

| Field | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Promise | What outcome do they sell? |
| User | Who is clearly targeted? |
| First value | How quickly can a user experience value? |
| Pricing | Plans, trial, discounts, expansion, enterprise route |
| Distribution | Store, SEO, community, sales, marketplace, paid |
| UX conventions | Patterns users expect in this category |
| Complaints | Repeated review/support pain |
| Trust gaps | Refunds, data loss, privacy, cancellation, support |
| Defensibility | Data, network, workflow lock-in, brand, ecosystem |

## Synthesis rules

- `research-1` — Do not copy competitor wording or structure. Convert observations into original product principles.
- `research-2` — Treat repeated complaints as product opportunities only when they align with a buyer willing to switch/pay.
- `research-3` — Separate table stakes from differentiators. Missing table stakes hurt trust; differentiators create pull.
- `research-4` — Look at weak competitors too; bad reviews expose category failure modes.
- `research-5` — Pricing norms reveal buyer expectation, not necessarily optimal price.
- `research-6` — If every competitor has the same feature, ask whether it is a requirement, a copied convention, or a trap.

## Output quality

A good research synthesis produces decisions:

- what we must include;
- what we intentionally omit;
- what we do better;
- what wedge gives us distribution;
- what evidence could disprove the plan.
