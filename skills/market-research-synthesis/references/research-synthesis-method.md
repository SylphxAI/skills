# Research Synthesis Method

## Source ledger

Create the ledger before writing the narrative.

| Field | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Claim ID | Stable link from recommendations to evidence |
| Source and publisher | Exact URL, document, interview, store, or dataset owner |
| Access date and scope | When observed; geography, locale, plan, currency, device, cohort |
| Evidence class | Direct observation, primary claim, attributed user report, inference, or judgment |
| Independence | Original source or repetition of another claim |
| Direction | Supports, contradicts, or complicates the working hypothesis |
| Confidence | High, medium, or low with a short reason |
| Limitation | Missing access, selection bias, stale date, small sample, or incomparable scope |

Do not promote an inference into an observed fact when summarizing.

## Source hierarchy

Choose the strongest sources available for the claim:

1. official product behavior, documentation, pricing, filings, changelog, or
   store listing for what the company currently offers or claims;
2. independently observable product behavior or measured dataset;
3. attributable user evidence with cohort and selection limits;
4. analyst, community, or comparison material for hypotheses and discovery;
5. anonymous or unattributable anecdotes only as weak leads.

Primary does not mean unbiased. Company pages are authoritative for current
published offers, not for customer outcomes or market demand. Review platforms
reveal failure modes, not population prevalence without a sampling model.

## Triangulation

For a material claim:

- find the closest primary evidence;
- seek an independent source that measures or observes the same mechanism;
- check whether scope, date, geography, plan, and user cohort match;
- record conflicting evidence and explain why it differs;
- lower confidence when corroboration is unavailable.

Repeated articles derived from the same announcement are one source family.
Counts without denominator, sampling method, or date are not market-size proof.

## Competitor set

Include enough variety to expose different mechanisms:

- category leaders for table stakes and distribution power;
- challengers for alternative positioning;
- niche winners for focused jobs or underserved cohorts;
- substitutes, including manual workflows and adjacent products;
- failed, abandoned, or low-rated examples for category failure modes.

Do not compare unlike plans, regions, billing periods, or product generations
without making the mismatch visible.

## Counterevidence sweep

Actively look for:

- users who reject the assumed problem or value metric;
- products that succeeded without the supposed table stake;
- products that failed despite the recommended feature or channel;
- complaints contradicted by behavior or concentrated in one cohort;
- switching, procurement, integration, trust, or migration costs;
- regulation or platform constraints that invalidate a tactic;
- survivorship, review, community, affiliate, and search-ranking bias.

The final recommendation should name what evidence would reverse it.

## Synthesis

Classify findings:

- **table stake** — absence blocks trust or basic category use;
- **convention** — common, but not yet proven necessary;
- **differentiator** — meaningful advantage for a named cohort;
- **wedge** — differentiated value plus a plausible acquisition or switching path;
- **trap** — copied behavior that adds cost, harm, or no defensible value;
- **unknown** — decision-relevant claim without adequate evidence.

Every proposed product or positioning choice should cite claim IDs for support,
counterevidence, confidence, and the smallest disconfirming test.

## Decision readiness checklist

Do not issue a recommendation until all applicable checks pass:

- the decision, alternatives, affected cohort, geography, time horizon, and
  irreversible consequences are explicit;
- each material factual claim resolves to the source ledger rather than an
  unattributed summary;
- direct observations, source claims, attributed reports, inferences, and
  judgments remain visibly distinct;
- at least one credible substitute and one credible counterexample were
  examined, not only named competitors;
- pricing, feature, review, and demand comparisons use compatible dates,
  regions, plans, currencies, and cohorts or disclose the mismatch;
- duplicated source families do not masquerade as independent corroboration;
- the recommendation identifies confidence, counterevidence, uncertainty,
  reversal conditions, and the cheapest decisive next test;
- a reader can reproduce the conclusion from the ledger without trusting the
  author’s narrative authority.
