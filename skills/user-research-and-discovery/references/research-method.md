# User research method

Use this reference to design and interpret generative or qualitative user
research. Adapt the method to participant risk, product maturity, decision
consequence, and evidence already available.

## Research contract

Define before recruitment:

- the decision and who will use the result;
- research questions, assumptions, and explicit non-questions;
- target users, contexts, behaviors, and material variation;
- method, session count rationale, recruitment and exclusion matrix;
- consent, recording, compensation, privacy, retention, accessibility,
  safeguarding, withdrawal, and incident handling;
- stopping conditions and evidence that would overturn the favored account.

The session count is a coverage decision, not a ritual number. Add participants
when they cover a material behavior, context, access need, risk, or unresolved
contradiction. Stop when the decision has adequate coverage and additional
sessions have low expected information value; do not claim statistical
representativeness or universal saturation.

## Method selection

| Question | Suitable evidence | Common failure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| What are users trying to achieve and why? | Contextual interview, observation, diary study | Asking only what feature they want |
| How is work actually performed? | Contextual inquiry, shadowing, artifact walkthrough | Treating recalled behavior as observed behavior |
| Can users understand or operate a proposed flow? | Concept or usability session with realistic tasks | Teaching the interface during the test |
| How does experience vary over time? | Diary or longitudinal study | One interview standing in for a changing journey |
| How common is a behavior or problem? | Appropriately sampled quantitative research | Counting a purposive qualitative sample |

Use mixed methods when different evidence classes answer different parts of the
decision. Do not call multiple weak methods triangulation unless they have
meaningfully different error structures.

## Recruitment and participant protection

- Recruit people whose behavior or context matches the research question, not
  only internal staff, power users, or easy respondents.
- Include access needs and adverse or edge conditions that can materially
  change the design.
- Avoid proxies when the affected person can participate safely. If a proxy is
  necessary, label proxy evidence.
- Minimize personally identifiable and sensitive data. Separate contact data
  from research data where practical and enforce retention and deletion.
- Make participation voluntary and withdrawal intelligible. Compensation must
  not depend on giving favorable feedback.
- Treat minors, health, finance, safety, employment, coercion, surveillance,
  and vulnerable populations as elevated-risk research requiring appropriate
  governance.

## Collection discipline

Pilot the guide and tasks. Begin with context and recent concrete experience.
Use open prompts, then neutral probes. Avoid leading language, hypothetical
feature voting, bundled questions, praise cues, and defending the design.

For every material session preserve:

- participant segment and relevant context without unnecessary identity;
- protocol version, researcher, date, channel, and deviations;
- consent and recording state;
- evidence locators for notes, clips, artifacts, or observations;
- observed behavior and participant language separately from interpretation.

## Analysis and confidence

Analyze across participants, not session by session alone:

1. Normalize evidence units without erasing context.
2. Code or group observations using definitions that can be revised.
3. Build themes only after checking supporting and contradictory cases.
4. Compare relevant segments and contexts; surface missing cells.
5. Trace every material finding to evidence and every recommendation to one or
   more findings.
6. Rate confidence from evidence directness, coverage, consistency,
   triangulation, researcher dependence, and consequence—not quotation count.

Use language proportional to evidence:

- **Observed** — directly seen in the study context.
- **Reported** — stated by participants but not directly observed.
- **Interpreted** — analytical explanation consistent with the evidence.
- **Hypothesized** — plausible account requiring more evidence.

Preserve negative cases. A finding may be segment-specific or unresolved. Do
not average contradictory needs into a fictional universal user.

## Decision handoff

Each recommendation must state the affected users and contexts, supporting and
contradicting evidence, confidence, expected decision consequence, and next
validation. Separate immediate usability defects from strategic opportunity,
policy constraint, operational gap, and open research question.

## Sources

- ISO, **ISO 9241-210:2019 Ergonomics of human-system interaction — Part 210:
  Human-centred design for interactive systems**,
  <https://www.iso.org/standard/77520.html>. Establishes human-centred design
  activities grounded in explicit understanding of users, tasks, and
  environments and evaluation against requirements.
- UK Government Service Manual, **User research**,
  <https://www.gov.uk/service-manual/user-research>. Practical public guidance
  for planning research, recruiting participants, choosing methods, conducting
  sessions, analysis, and sharing findings.
- UK Government Service Manual, **Plan user research for your service**,
  <https://www.gov.uk/service-manual/user-research/plan-user-research-for-your-service>.
  Guidance on research questions, participant groups, methods, ethics, and
  iterative planning.
